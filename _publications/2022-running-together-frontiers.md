We examined how recreational runners benefit from running with others to maintain a consistent training regimen over time. Using three-wave, individual-level panel data of more than 800 runners from the ABS project ("Always Keep Active"), we found that increases over time in the number of co-runners are related to increases in the number of weekly running sessions. The probability of turning up at the Seven Hills Run was positively related to the number of equally or less competent co-runners, and to the number with whom respondents also discussed important matters on a frequent basis. Our recreational athletes differed in the extent to which they expressed social motivations to run. However, among these athletes, the positive impact of sports partners on sport outcomes did not depend on the importance of social motives. Our study demonstrates that social networks play an important role in maintaining a consistent training habit and in reaching set goals (i.e., participating in a race).
